Skills are the heart of Neoquest. Not all skill trees provide the same level of power, and that's okay. Trees tend to spike in power at different points of the game, so you can choose whether you'd like to trivialize the early-midgame, or whether you want to completely crush the endgame. Each tree is still more than capable of getting you through the game.

Fire gets a bad rep in pretty much every Neoquest guide that I have seen, not that I have seen many new Neoquest guides have come out recently. Regardless, I agree in some ways that you get better value by investing in other trees, but Fire is by no means a weak tree. The power you get early on is actually very significant, and it stays effective for the rest of the game even if it falls off a little towards the end. Grinding is very quick at any stage of the game because of your sheer damage output from skills and weapons, though you do need to keep an eye on your health bar during boss fights because this tree provides exactly zero defensive power.

A key thing to note about this tree is that using a Fire weapon at all stages of the game is recommended for the weapon + skill synergy.

Skill 1: Fire Weapons
Fire Weapons on its own is a very powerful skill, and its damage when combined with Firepower is more than enough to breeze your way through the early to midgame with zero issues. In theory, you could max Fire Weapons and Firepower to unlock your 14 fire damage procs by level 11. That's pretty insane considering the amount of HP that enemies have and the amount of damage that your basic attacks do at that point of the game. Of course, some might argue that it's a little more sensible to go Fire Ball and get the burst damage at the start of your random encounters, or even go one step further and spread evenly to get Wall of Flame active early on as well. As you can see, there are already many ways for skill investment to go so early on. Regardless, Fire Weapons is absurdly strong in the early game, scales well into the midgame, and then tapers off to a reasonable damage boost in the endgame, though you should note that it tends to get resisted quite a bit by endgame bosses. By all counts, it is still a very respectable skill, but I have just one reservation about it, which is that it is strictly inferior to its Ice counterpart, though we will discuss that later.

Rating: 7/10

Skill 2: Firepower
I think the Neoquest devs were either really lazy or ran out of ideas when coming up with this skill because its only purpose seems to be making you waste points before you can reap the benefits of the more powerful skills further down the tree, and it's also required to get the full power of Fire Weapons. Technically, it does boost the power of Fire Ball as well, but you would literally never know because you cannot invest in Fire Ball without investing in Firepower first. The skill makes very little sense. Perhaps the devs thought maxing Fire Ball or Wall of Flame early on was too powerful. There is nothing that would make you want to invest in this skill besides it being a requirement to get to the better skills. I don't think that should really be how it works, so my opinion of this skill is quite low. It would be higher if Fire skills had greater damage outputs than their Ice counterparts, but I think you already know that's not the case.

Rating: 4/10

Skill 3: Fire Ball
Fire Ball is a pretty standard castable fire skill. It does a good chunk more damage than your basic attack in the early to midgame, serving as a bit of burst to start off your battles, but the cooldown at lower skill levels basically means that you will only get to cast it once per random encounter, and at higher levels, your basic attack with Fire Weapons procs and the Inferno Robe will easily outdamage it since it maxes out at 45 damage at level 10. In addition, much like Fire Weapons, it tends to get resisted more towards the later part of the game, particularly against bosses. Nonetheless, it serves its purpose well for the majority of the game, and it's also a prerequisite for Wall of Flame, so it's really not optional.

Rating: 6/10

Skill 4: Wall of Flame
This skill is the crown jewel of the Fire skill tree, and whether you invest in Fire or not depends entirely on whether you want to use this skill or not. It is very weak early because the value per skill point is distributed more heavily towards the last five levels of the skill, but it wreaks havoc at level 10, dealing a massive 22 fire damage for every basic attack the enemy lands on you. Unlike many other skills in this game, there is no RNG here. What this means is that enemies will whittle down their own health bars whenever they hit you, and the damage adds up very quickly. It's a passive, so you don't need to spend a turn to cast it, and it provides a relatively reliable source of damage against bosses who don't spam elemental skills too. You might argue that enemies in the endgame start using more elemental skills, but keep in mind that every single enemy in the game uses basic attacks, which are what the game is generally balanced around. Combine this skill with your basic attack damage, Fire Weapons procs, Blast Furnace procs, Inferno Robe damage, and I think it should be very clear why Fire is the most damaging skill tree in the game. Mandatory to invest in if you are running Fire.

Rating: 10/10

Glacier Strike

  • This damage can be resisted and decreased

Ice is a very versatile skill tree and personally one of my favourites in the game. There are two main types of Ice builds, one which is focused on basic attacks, and the other around Glacier Strike. Both are entirely viable and spike at different parts of the game. Some might argue that Glacier Strike is unreliable because of stun interruptions, or that it wastes points in Heart of Ice, but it also has upsides that I believe make up for that. Ice is also a heavily offence-focused tree, but both builds have to go through Heart of Ice, so there is an essential element of defense as well.

Skill 1: Ice Weapons
At first glance, this skill looks identical to Fire Weapons, yet I would consider it possibly one of the best skills in the game. Why is that? Because for just 10 skill points, you can max this skill out and start dealing 14 ice damage at level 4 with a very high proc rate. Your basic attacks are dealing low single digit damage at this point by the way. Keep in mind that this skill deals additional damage on top of your basic attack. This means that in the first roughly twelve levels of the game before you get your first weapon upgrade, this skill adds multiple times your basic attack damage on every proc. As you hit your first weapon upgrade, this scales down to about 1x-1.5x your basic attack, and then in the endgame, somewhere around 0.3x your basic attack. As you can see, the value of the skill does diminish over time, but the sheer amount of early game power that it provides for minimal investment cannot be overstated. In contrast, Fire Weapons requires a whole 10 points more in Firepower to match the damage. This skill is as efficient as early game damage gets. It also isn't entirely wasted on Glacier Strike builds because there are turns where Snowball will not be available to cast on the second turn of your Glacier Strike, so you will basic attack occasionally.

Rating: 10/10

Skill 2: Heart of Ice
Heart of Ice, in a very similar way to Ice Weapons, is a skill that starts off incredibly strong and then dials back to normal levels of usefulness as you progress later in the game. This is because it only prevents enemies from basic attacking, and enemies in the early game often do very little else than that, while enemies in the mid to lategame start hitting you with elemental blasts and drains. Again though, Heart of Ice has a very high proc rate for what is essentially a stun in many parts of the game, and it's available as the second skill, meaning that no points go into unnecessary skills before you get to it since it synergizes well with Ice Weapons on landing basic attacks. This skill will save you from a lot of damage in all your battles because even endgame enemies still use basic attacks. Overall, a very solid skill.

Rating: 8/10

Skill 3: Snowball Snowball is the Ice equivalent of Fire Ball. At max level it even does the same damage. However, unlike Fireball, which you probably want to stop using in the endgame, Snowball serves a very specific purpose of not wasting your second turn while Glacier Strike is charging. When maxed out, its cooldown slots very well into the Glacier Strike attack pattern, requiring just one basic attack between Glacier Strikes to become available again for the second turn. Essentially, Snowball helps Glacier Strike transform from an okay skill into a very good full build. Of course, things down play out this well until you get to higher skill levels, which is where the other Ice skills come into play. You would never invest in Snowball unless you plan to invest in Glacier Strike, but it serves its purpose so well that I have a very high opinion of it.

Rating: 9/10

Skill 4: Glacier Strike
Glacier Strike is quite a polarizing (no pun intended) skill. Some people swear that it is useless and that your basic attacks and skill + weapon procs outdamage it in the three turns it takes to cast, while others swear by its reliable hit rate and burst damage. I am part of the latter, as I've breezed through multiple Evil mode runs without any issue using a Glacier Strike build. Its weakest point is definitely the early game, where it tends to get resisted a lot and Snowball's cooldown isn't low enough to easily cast it again without wasting the second turn. You might want to consider just basic attacking at this point because of how powerful Ice's first two skills are. However, once you reach around level 34 and the Ice tree is maxed, then you start really reaping the benefits. Snowball slots into the second turn of your charge, which means you only spend one turn charging Glacier Strike rather than two, greatly reducing the damage gap between the basic attack build and Glacier Strike. The hit rate at max level is also quite high, making it a reliable choice for bossing, especially those that you cannot gain a level lead against. Also, and I would consider this a decently important point, Glacier Strike is the only way that you will see your own character dealing three digit damage in a single hit. There's just something very satisfying about seeing yourself deal damage that exceeds your normal attack damage multiple times.

Rating: 9/10

The Shock Skill Tree

  • This stun can be resisted
  • Proc rate increases with level
  • Stun duration does not increase with investment - always stuns for one turn

Skill level 10: moderate proc rate

  • When an attack successfully connects with an enemy, it has a chance to proc this skill and reduce the enemy's defense
  • This skill can be resisted
  • Proc rate and defense reduction increase with level
Fortitude
  • Increases your chance to resist elemental blasts (fire, ice, shock)
  • Has no effect on poison or drain
  • Resistance rate increases with skill level
Shockwave
  • When an attack successfully connects with an enemy, it has a chance to proc this skill, stunning the enemy for one turn and inflicting damage
  • The stun and damage can both be reduced and resisted
  • The stun and damage each get a separate resist roll
  • Proc rate and damage increase with skill level
  • Stun duration does not increase with investment - always stuns for one turn

Skill level 10: 1-turn stun + 22 damage, high proc rate

The Shock skill tree is, I believe, widely considered both the most powerful and the most essential skill tree in the game, and for very good reasons. Stun is one of the most powerful status effects and the most powerful form of impairment in this game. It simply means that the enemy is unable to take any action on their turn with no exceptions. This also makes stun arguably one of the best defensive skills as well because enemies cannot hit you when they cannot act. Shock comes with not one, but two skills that can separately inflict this effect, one which also damages the enemy on proc, and the stun duration can stack! That's not all though. This tree also contains the absolute most important defense skill in the game, raising your resistance against the nasty elemental blasts that are very common in the endgame. With such a combination of defense and offense, Shock provides what I would consider the most enjoyable endgame experience, spiking heavily around the midgame when Shockwave and Fortitude fully come online, and continuing strongly right to the end.

Skill 1: Shock Weapons
Stun is the most powerful status effect available to you as a player. This skill lets you stun the enemy for one turn. That's it. It's that simple, and also that effective. Usually, inflicting a status effect on the enemy is thought of as an offensive action, but stun is actually a purely defensive skill, preventing any follow up from your enemy on the turn. As it turns out, preventing damage ends up being more powerful than healing in the endgame where even normal enemies can regularly drain you for 100 HP. Of course, stun does suffer the same drawback as any other status effect, which is that it can and does tend to be resisted more later in the game, not by the monsters, but by the bosses, which are by far the deadliest enemies. Regardless of that, Shock Weapons is still a very respectable skill, and it is a prerequisite for the even more important skills down the tree, so maxing this skill is fully recommended.

Rating: 9/10

Skill 2: Disable
Disable is an interesting skill if you actually understand what it does. I did not fully understand what defense actually did in this game until I read neoquest.guide actually, so I highly recommend checking out that site. Anyway, Disable lowers the enemy's defense when it procs, making your enemy easier to hit. The issue with this skill is that players get a random, watered down version of this skill compared to bosses. When Jahbal or the Guardian of Spectral Magic hit you with this skill, they hardly miss on their next attacks. When you proc this skill, it's hard to see a difference in hit rate even knowing that Disable is active, and so I find it hard to give this skill meaningful credit. Is it meaningful over the course of an entire battle? Maybe, but I even find it a little hard to believe that because it gets resisted a lot to begin with against bosses, and you weren't missing that much against normal enemies anyway. By far the weakest skill in the Shock tree, but perhaps that's just because of how powerful the rest of them are. You have to invest in this skill anyway, but just don't expect much from it.

Rating: 4/10

Skill 3: Fortitude
I know I talked a lot about how stun is the most powerful defensive status effect, but Fortitude is the most powerful defensive skill in the game. Fortitude provides passive elemental resistance on every turn, meaning that it is always operating at full effectiveness whether you can act or not. This is an important distinction from Shock Weapons and Shockwave because you will eat 3-turn + 70 damage stunblasts over the course of the game, and this skill provides crucial defense against further 100+ elemental damage follow up from the enemy during that time. Resisting one enemy attack is often the difference between living and dying in a boss fight, but the skill is also just useful in random encounters in the mid to lategame against enemies who like to use elemental skills, often saving you an entire potion's worth of HP at once. It's even arguable that skipping Shockwave to put more points into Fortitude if you don't have enough remaining points is a sensible thing to do, despite Shockwave being the best skill in the game. Elemental resistance is important in this game. Do not forget it.

Rating: 9/10

Skill 4: Shockwave
I don't think it is an exaggeration to suggest that Shockwave may be the single most powerful skill in the game. In fact, I am arguing exactly that. When you combine the most powerful status effect with damage that exceeds the damage of other trees' pure damage skill procs by 50%, package it into a single skill, and then give it a good proc rate, you should have an easy time seeing why I think this skill is broken. Shock's only offensive ability comes from this skill, and it is still more than enough. The stun and the damage have separate resist rolls, meaning that if one part fails, the other can still hit. If that still wasn't enough, Shockwave's proc rate and damage are not affected by weapon synergy, meaning that this is the only on-hit skill in the game that operates at full effectiveness regardless of the weapon you are running. It has no weaknesses besides being resistable and requiring investment in three other skills to access it. There is no better skill in the game.

Rating: 11/10

  • This skill does not have any proc requirements
  • Amount healed and proc rate increase with skill level

Lifesteal
  • When an attack successfully connects with the enemy, it has a chance to proc this skill and heal the user for a moderate amount of health as lifesteal
  • Despite the skill name, this skill does not scale based on damage inflicted
  • Amount healed increases with skill level

Skill level 10: Lifesteal up to 10 HP per proc

Resurrection
  • When the user's HP drops to or below 0 in battle, they have a chance to be revived at 50% HP and continue the battle in the current state.

I am unsure whether this skill can proc multiple times in one battle because I have never fully invested in it, nor do I recommend doing so.

  • Resurrection rate increases with skill level

The Life skill tree is the single most powerful tree in the early to midgame, and it is not remotely close. The advantage of the other defense-oriented trees, Shock and Spectral, is that they scale based on how much damage that they can potentially prevent an enemy from dealing each turn, meaning that they reach their full potential much later into the game. On the other hand, Life provides consistent, raw healing power that comes online extremely early (full potential by level 24!) and quickly makes way for your second skill tree. Even better, Field Medic and Lifesteal don't rely on weapon synergy, so you can reap the full benefits of Life while completely focusing on your secondary tree. However, by no means am I trying to imply that Life is weak later on and that you will get carried by your other skill tree. Sure, it falls off a little turn-to-turn when enemies can deal more than 1/3 of your HP bar with one skill, but you will still heal a very significant amount over the course of each battle, even more so if you run the Moonstone Staff with it.

There is one thing that we need to talk about though. If you invest in Life early on, expect the earlygame to be trivial... but also extremely slow. Unlike any other skill tree in the game, Life does not possess a single damage-dealing skill. What this means is that you will be relying solely on your basic attack damage for roughly the first half of the game, making battles take far more turns on average. This is ultimately of no consequence unless you really want to speed through the game for some reason, but be warned if you are easily bored.

Skill 1: Life Weapons
Life Weapons follows the power curve of its skill tree to a tee. If we use the base values of each healing potion for comparison, Life Weapons provides a healing boost of 100%, 33%, 16.66%, 11.11%, and 8.33%, and 6.66% as we move up the potion tiers. That's a lot of extra earlygame healing from Weak and Standard Healing Potions if you couldn't tell. I consider the overworld healing to be somewhat inconsequential, but it does provide a bit of value over time. An overall solid skill that is necessary to get deeper into the skill tree anyway.

Rating: 8/10

Skill 2: Field Medic
Field Medic is one of your two in-combat healing skills, and while it doesn't amount to too much on its own, it contributes to the turn-to-turn healing system you get by running full Life. Like all Life skills, its greatest impact is in the earlygame when that 4 HP is a decent chunk of your health, but HP is HP at any point in the game.

Rating: 7/10

If you see the game like I do, then Resurrection seems to be largely a useless skill because for the vast majority of the game, you should never be in a position where dying is possible. If you are dying, then you have likely done something wrong: either not watching your HP bar correctly during boss fights, or not having enough levels over the regular enemies. If you are running offense-oriented skill trees, then there will be times where potions are scarce, but then Resurrection doesn't apply to those cases anyway. In the worst case, you can usually walk back. However, I propose a single scenario where Resurrection is not a complete waste of skill points: when you are running a Life build on InSaNe mode. With the investment of a single skill point in Resurrection, you buy yourself a very small lifeline on the off chance that something happens when fighting the big three bosses.
